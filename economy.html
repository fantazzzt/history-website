Economic Decline

Spanning 284-750 CE

One of the most important conclusions that recent historical analysis on Late Antiquity has been that the period is not one of sole decline. While the political integrity of Western Roman bureaucratic institutions had been atrophied, cultural and religious transformations were important developments that contradicted the notion of a general decline. Partially, this can be applied to the economic side of Late Antiquity, that the period of Late Antiquity saw states grow on their own economic networks, while others saw decline.

This of course will depend on the perspective of each country. For instance, the barbarians that resided near the empire typically saw greater economic development than barbarians that were farther from the borders of Rome. Roughly, this is due to the many trading initiatives between Roman forts and barbarian encampments that were by the border. Archaeological data show that not only were the tools better, but invariably the agricultural practices also must have been better. It was typical for barbarian agriculture to be mobile: rarely was the soil turned upon itself to feedback nutrients into the environment, causing land to go sterile after a few harvests.

For Rome, the economy is a different story. It is true that the era after Diocletian's reforms saw economic growth in some years, but overall, we see the decline of Roman cities. It becomes even more apparent once we see the state of the Roman army in the late Roman Empire. Simply put, the late Roman Empire suffered from significant manpower shortages. Hence, the army would have to be supplemented with barbarian manpower on the occasion. Additionally, the current era called for different strategies than past systems. Rather than the aggressive conquering legion of Trajan in the principate, the Late Roman Empire was designed for security, meaning the organization of armies was designed to protect Roman borders. It becomes evident that when the economic decline of the late Roman Empire was in full swing, the resources used to defend the empire also declined. This does not suggest that barbarians outside the empire always took advantage of Rome when given the chance. Still, there are significant and powerful groups that put pressure on the empire's borders that make it difficult for Rome to protect all of its possessions. Additionally, the late Roman Empire also saw the rise of the Sasanid Empire, which was a significantly more formidable opponent than the previous Partian Empire. As such, many instances of Roman garrisons were reassigned to the east, again allowing the plundering of the Roman heartland for opportunist groups. Compounded with the rise of the Huns meant that the Romans would face meaningful threats to its economic dominance.

That being said, life after the fall of the Western Roman Empire in former provinces would not have likely been too different than previous living. The states that succeeded the Roman Empire often maintained the institutions and bureaucracy to a similar degree to the Roman Empire, meaning that these states were centralized and effective in exercising state power. For example, King Odoacer, the man responsible for ending the Western Roman Empire politically, was credited as a competent ruler, with many construction projects that attribute to a centralized state. Thus, contrary to misconceptions about the Fall of Rome, Feudalism was not an economic system that was prevalent for many centuries after 476. In fact, the Eastern Roman Empire would continue on operating on a system that never truly resembled Feudalism even throughout the Medieval Age.
