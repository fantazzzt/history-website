Sassanids in Late Antiquity

The Sassanid Empire, which spanned from 224 to 651 AD, was the last great Persian Empire before the rise of Islam. Established in present-day Iran, it was a formidable counterpart to the Roman Empire, resulting in numerous Roman-Persian Wars.

Founded by Ardashir I after overthrowing the Parthian Empire, the Sassanids were staunch believers in Zoroastrianism. They regarded themselves as the true successors to the Achaemenids and strived to restore the greatness of the past Persian empires.

Art, culture, and science flourished under Sassanid rule. This era saw remarkable advancements in architecture, literature, philosophy, medicine, and astronomy, which would later influence Islamic civilization.

Architecturally, the Sassanids introduced new city planning concepts, innovative fortifications, and majestic palatial complexes. Their statecraft, refined over centuries, was a subject of admiration and study. The empire's decline came with the Islamic Conquest in 651 AD, marking an end to Zoroastrian dominance in Persia.
